Two deep-dives already published in this corpus have reached for the <PartReference id="gazzew-boba-u4t" /> as a comparison point without ever explaining it on its own terms. [The Durock T1 piece](/article/durock-t1-deep-dive) used the U4T's D-shaped plateau to locate the T1's sharper, faster-resolving bump. [The Drop Holy Panda X piece](/article/drop-holy-panda-x-deep-dive) cited its dry factory ship as the counterpoint to the HPX's asymmetric lube job. Both citations were accurate, and neither stopped to ask the more interesting question: why does a switch built from the same stem and housing as its own quieter sibling end up sounding — and feeling — like a different switch entirely.

## One stem, two finishes

Gazzew's Boba line has run two versions of the same core switch since the <Mono>Boba U4T</Mono> reached NovelKeys in June 2020: the Boba U4, built with foam dampeners inside the housing to quiet both ends of the stroke, and the U4T, the full-sound counterpart that ships without them. The stem geometry is shared. The nylon top and bottom housings are shared. The pronounced, rounded bump positioned high in the stroke — the trait every Boba review leads with — is shared too. The only variable Gazzew changed between the two switches is what sits inside the housing walls at top-out and bottom-out.

That is an unusually clean natural experiment for a hobby that rarely gets one. Most switch comparisons cross several variables at once — stem material, spring, housing resin — and any acoustic difference has to be untangled from all of them. The U4 and U4T isolate a single change: foam, present or absent. Everything the U4T is known for — the full-bodied thud, the bottom-out that "rings" instead of landing quietly — traces back to that one subtraction.

## The bump: why it sits where it does

The U4T's tactile event is a D-shaped bump: a steep climb on the leading edge, a flat plateau across the top rather than a sharp peak, and a controlled descent back to baseline pressure. That shape is a direct consequence of stem geometry — the POM stem carries a rounded shoulder that maintains contact with the leaf across an extended arc rather than crossing it at a steep angle, the way a Cherry MX Brown's stem does. A steep-angle crossing produces a narrow spike, over almost as soon as it starts. A rounded shoulder holding contact longer produces a plateau — the finger reads "I am in the bump" for a measurable stretch of travel rather than "I crossed a bump" in an instant.

Gazzew positions that plateau high in the 4.0mm stroke, in the upper third of the travel. The tactile event resolves early, and the rest of the downstroke — from the bump's back edge down to the 4.0mm floor — is functionally a quiet linear glide. That's a deliberate trade: placing the plateau lower, or stretching it further down the stroke, would blur the bump into the bottom-out, the way a <PartReference id="drop-holy-panda-x" /> blends its own rounded arc into one continuous gesture. The U4T keeps the two events distinct instead — bump, then a stretch of nothing, then a full-bodied landing.

The spring backs that separation up more than its numbers first suggest. At 62g actuation and 68g bottom-out, the U4T's spread is only 6 grams — among the narrowest of any tactile in this publication's comparison set, and, as the T1 deep-dive already noted, roughly half the spread the <PartReference id="durock-t1" /> runs across its own 55g-into-67g curve. The U4T isn't asking the spring to keep loading meaningfully after the bump resolves; it's asking for the heaviest single moment of force right at the plateau, then coasting on a spring that's nearly done climbing. The heaviest breakpoint in the comparison set, delivered early, then very little left to prove.

## What the foam actually did

  src="/article-viz/gazzew-boba-u4t-deep-dive/foam-removed.svg"

  caption="Same stem, same housing, same bump — the dampeners are the entire difference between a quiet Boba and a loud one."
  accent="coral"

A foam dampener does one specific mechanical job: it inserts a compressible layer between two hard surfaces at the moment they'd otherwise meet directly. On the Boba U4, that layer sits at the top-out, cushioning the stem's return against the housing ceiling, and at the bottom-out, cushioning its descent against the housing floor. Both contacts still happen — what changes is the instant after contact. Instead of two hard surfaces ringing against each other and radiating that vibration as sound, the foam absorbs the impact energy and converts most of it to heat. The keystroke still registers the same travel and spring resistance; it just doesn't make much noise doing it.

Strip the foam out and nothing about the actuation changes — the U4T isn't a different mechanism, it's the same mechanism with an acoustic absorber removed. The stem's top-out and bottom-out contacts are now hard-surface-on-hard-surface, and the nylon housing itself becomes the thing doing the ringing. Nylon is a comparatively soft engineering plastic as housings go, so it doesn't ring as brightly as polycarbonate would in the same configuration — the result isn't a sharp click, it's a deep, contained thud. The housing resonates at a lower register than a stiffer resin would, but it resonates, because nothing intercepts the impact before it has the chance to.

That's the entire mechanism behind the U4T's reputation. Builders describe the switch as sounding "full" or "rich" compared to its silenced sibling, and the plain engineering answer is that the U4 was never quiet because its housing couldn't ring — it was quiet because Gazzew put something in the way before it could. The U4T just took the obstacle out.

<Callout type="note" title="Not the same lever as a silent-linear O-ring">
  It's worth distinguishing this from the O-ring mod some builders run on linears. A stem O-ring sits above the housing and cushions top-out only, added after the fact by the builder. Gazzew's foam dampeners are a factory-fitted, in-housing design choice covering both ends of the stroke, and removing them (the U4-to-U4T difference) is a different move from adding a ring — subtraction of a factory feature rather than addition of an aftermarket one.
</Callout>

## The spring rattle nobody warns you about

The U4T's factory lube is real but narrow: Gazzew pre-lubes the rails, the surfaces where the stem slides against the housing walls, but leaves the spring untouched. That's a defensible choice on its own terms — over-lubing a spring can gum coils together and change its behavior — but it has a predictable side effect. A stock spring, uncoated and unconstrained, tends to chatter against itself on the upstroke as it decompresses, especially once the foam that would have otherwise muffled a stray vibration is gone. The result is a thin, metallic rattle riding on top of the switch's low thud — audible on a quiet board, more noticeable precisely because the U4T's acoustic signature is otherwise so deep and clean.

The fix the community settled on is mechanical, not exotic: a thin coat of a low-viscosity oil applied by hand to each spring, enough to damp coil-on-coil contact without adding drag to the compression stroke. It isn't a factory step, and it isn't optional in the way rail lube is — a U4T straight out of the bag will very likely need it before the acoustic picture matches what the switch is capable of. Builders who skip it are, in effect, judging the switch on an incomplete build. Contrast that with [the Gateron Oil King deep dive](/article/gateron-oil-king-deep-dive), where the factory tune already extends past the rails to the stem legs — still no spring treatment, but a broader factory lube footprint than the U4T's rails-only approach at a similar price point.

<Callout type="warn" title="Ordering the wrong Boba">
  Because the U4 and U4T share a name, a housing, and a listing family on most vendor pages, it's an easy switch to order wrong — especially for a buyer scanning specs quickly and registering only "Boba" and "tactile." If the goal is the full-sound thud this piece describes, the "T" is load-bearing. The plain U4 is a different acoustic proposition by design, not a lesser version of the same one.
</Callout>

Set against the two other tactiles this publication has already covered, the U4T occupies a distinct corner: the heaviest actuation weight in the set at 62g, paired with the narrowest spread to bottom-out at just 6 grams. The <PartReference id="durock-t1" /> runs the opposite pattern — a lighter 55g actuation that climbs a full 12 grams by the time the stroke bottoms out, closing most of that gap by the floor of the press even though it starts well behind. The Holy Panda X splits the difference at 60g into 67g, a 7-gram spread that sits close to the U4T's own number despite a shorter 3.4mm travel and a different bump shape entirely.

The practical read: the T1 asks for escalating commitment through the stroke; the U4T asks for its heaviest commitment up front, then gets out of the way. Neither is more "correct" — they're different answers to the same brief of making a tactile feel substantial rather than casual. The U4T reads as a distinct, locatable event followed by a quiet finish; the T1 reads as a press that keeps costing more the deeper it goes. For a tactile built on an entirely different mechanical lineage, [the Gateron Lanes deep dive](/article/gateron-lanes-tactile-deep-dive) traces an Alps-derived stepped-leg bump that has nothing in common with the U4T's rounded-shoulder plateau — worth reading as a reminder that "tactile" covers more than one mechanism.

<Source href="https://theremingoat.com">ThereminGoat's review</Source> of the U4T lines up with that read, describing it as one of the more distinctly voiced tactiles on the market — a switch reviewers reach for specifically because the bump and the bottom-out both register clearly, rather than one overshadowing the other.

## Who the U4T is for

The U4T rewards a typist who wants a tactile switch that also functions as an acoustic centerpiece — a board built to be heard as well as felt. It's a poor match for anyone building toward a quiet office setup; the entire design premise is a housing allowed to ring, and no amount of case dampening will make a U4T sound like a silenced switch without defeating the reason to buy it. It's a strong match for a builder coming from the plain <Mono>Boba U4</Mono> who found the silenced version's restraint disappointing.

Builders should budget for the spring-oiling pass as part of the install, not as an optional upgrade — the switch as it ships isn't the switch as it's meant to be heard. And the housing choice deserves acoustic company: an all-nylon build already leans low and contained, so a case and plate that add brightness elsewhere in the stack, rather than muting the U4T further, will let its actual character come through.

## What to watch next

The U4T has held its spec — 62g/68g, nylon/nylon, POM, 4.0mm — since its June 2020 release, and it remains in production through NovelKeys without a revision. That stability, alongside the T1's own six-year unchanged run, points at something durable about the heavy-tactile corner of the market: once a factory finds a spring tune and a housing pairing that reads as "serious" rather than merely "stiff," there's little incentive to touch it. The open question is whether Gazzew ever extends factory lube to the spring itself — the most obvious remaining gap between the U4T's factory tune and the full-lube standard the Holy Panda X has since set for the category. [The HMX Cloud deep dive](/article/hmx-cloud-deep-dive) covers the same question from the linear side of the catalog — a similarly unglamorous switch that climbed the tracker on tuning discipline rather than a headline material.
