The word used to describe an outcome. A builder would finish a tray
mount, drop in a set of switches they'd spent two hours lubing with
a 1mm brush, and discover — sometimes — that the board they'd built
sounded the way the forum threads had promised. The sound was the
reward, and the reward wasn't guaranteed. A decade later the
language has flipped. "Thock" isn't what a well-built keyboard
happens to do anymore; it's what a $150 prebuilt is engineered to
do at the factory, before a single enthusiast touches it.

## From discovery to specification

In 2016 a thocky board was a sentence with caveats. Owners
described the lube job, the case foam they'd improvised from craft
felt, the plate material they'd swapped after a week of A/B
testing. The sound was downstream of decisions a builder made by
hand, in private, and the relationship between any one decision
and the final acoustic profile was something the hobby was still
working out in long forum threads. Two builders with nominally
identical parts lists would end up with audibly different boards,
and the gap between them was where the craft lived.

The vendor pitches of the era reflected that. Group-buy interest
checks led with the case design, the plate options, the colorway.
Sound was implied — the way a high-end speaker brand implies
fidelity — but the brand didn't underwrite it. The builder did.
Boards shipped, the builder did the work, and the sound was the
builder's outcome to chase.

In 2026 the same pitch leads with the acoustics. The product page
for a midweight prebuilt now itemizes the dampening stack, names
the switches as factory-lubed by SKU, calls out the plate
material and the gasket geometry by spec, and — increasingly —
hosts a sound test in the page header before the photography of
the case finishes loading. The vendor is asserting the acoustic
result, not implying it. That's an industrial shift, not a
marketing one. The vendor can only assert what the factory can
deliver consistently.

## The four-layer stack went standard

Four moves stacked on top of each other to make that assertion
defensible.

The first is at the switch. Factory lubing went from a
sometimes-it's-there, sometimes-it's-uneven curiosity to a
quality bar that mid-tier brands now compete on. The thock
[lubing-101 guide](/article/lubing-101) covers what hand-lubing
buys a builder; what changed in the last two years is how much of
that gap factory work now closes. Brands that built their
reputation on the discipline — most visibly HMX, whose [Cloud
deep-dive](/article/hmx-cloud-deep-dive) argues that HMX's edge
isn't material novelty but rather the boring industrial answer of
"do the lubing well, at volume, every time" — are now what readers
recommend by default for a builder who doesn't want to lube. The
<Mono>Gateron Oil King</Mono> is the linear-sound exhibit B: its
signature acoustic profile is an engineered outcome of a specific
factory-lube viscosity matched to a specific housing tolerance,
not a happy accident that hobbyists discovered after the fact.
The [Oil King deep-dive](/article/gateron-oil-king-deep-dive)
walks through how deliberate that pairing is. Even Cherry, the
incumbent whose decade-long reputation for dry, scratchy stock
switches had become a running joke, shipped MX2A specifically to
close the factory-lube gap — the
[MX2A revision piece](/article/cherry-mx2a-revision) is the
clearest sign that the rest of the industry has redefined the
baseline.

The second is at the plate and case interface. Gasket mount
moved from a high-end custom flex into a mid-tier default,
which means the plate's interaction with the case isn't being
left to the builder anymore. The vendor picks the gasket
material, the compression height, and the plate flex profile,
and stamps a spec on it. The builder receives a sound the
vendor underwrote.

The third is dampening as a stack rather than a single layer.
The current generation of mid-tier prebuilts ships with a
silicone pad in the bottom case, foam tape between PCB and
plate, and a poron strip lining the case walls — three layers
chosen for what each one absorbs. The [Mode Sonnet build
piece](/article/building-mode-sonnet-with-oil-kings) walks
through what that looks like when a builder assembles it
deliberately; the new thing in 2026 is that a prebuilt at half
the price ships the same architecture out of the box, just with
the gasket count and the plate alloy ticked down a grade.

The fourth is the keycap default. PBT doubleshot is now the
keycap a mid-tier vendor ships stock. The contribution to the
sound is real but is often the least-discussed: doubleshot PBT
at 1.5mm wall thickness has an acoustic footprint distinct from
ABS at 1.2mm, and the shift in stock keycaps is part of why
prebuilts at the $150 tier sound the way they do without any
keycap swap.

<Callout type="note" title="The vocabulary settled">
  "Thock" is the deep, full-bodied bottom-out — the sound the
  hobby chases. "Clack" is brighter and higher in the spectrum,
  typically from a thinner plate, a stiffer case, or shallower
  switches. "Marble" is the rounded, mid-pitched profile some
  COPE-foam stacks generate. "Popcorn" is the loose, uneven
  acoustic where individual key sounds don't sit on a shared
  pitch. The four words used to be interchangeable in
  enthusiast hands; the vendor-spec era has needed them to mean
  separable things, and they now mostly do.
</Callout>

## What changes when the win condition becomes the floor

The interesting consequence isn't that more boards sound good.
It's what happens to the rest of the buying decision when the
acoustic outcome is no longer the variable. A reader picking a
$150 prebuilt in 2022 was effectively also picking how much
modding time they were signing up for; in 2026 that's largely
priced into the SKU. The decision compresses onto layout,
typing feel (gasket density, plate flex, switch character), and
aesthetics. That's a healthier shape for the hobby's gateway
tier — readers who used to bounce off the modding step now stay,
and the modders who used to differentiate on sound are now
differentiating further up the stack.

The shift also reframes the customs side. A $300 custom build
in 2026 isn't competing with the $150 prebuilt on whether it
sounds good — both will. It's competing on whether it sounds
specific. The mid-tier delivers a defensibly good thock at a
defensibly low variance; the high end's pitch is that it
delivers a particular thock the builder chose, which is a
different value proposition. Some of the old customs market has
to recompose itself around that. Not all of it will.

## What we're watching

Three threads run from here.

The first is the sub-$100 tier. Most of the four-layer stack is
shipping at $130–$180 now; the question is when, and from
whom, it reaches $90. The bottleneck is probably the gasket
mount — the others are already cheap at volume. A wired,
plastic-cased gasket-mount prebuilt with factory-lubed
switches and a foam stack at $99 would close the last gap in
the ladder. Several Chinese OEMs are within striking distance;
none has shipped one yet.

The second is whether plate and case materials become the next
design lever now that switches are figured out. If the switch
question is settled at the factory, the differentiation moves
to the structure around it — polycarbonate plates with
specified flex curves, FR4 layups, multi-layer aluminum-with-
brass-insert constructions. Some of this is already happening
on the customs side; what mid-tier picks up from it will tell
us where the next round of acoustic competition lands.

The third is what happens to the high-end's acoustic edge if
the mid-tier keeps closing the gap. A $600 group-buy custom
can't sustainably sell on "sounds better than the $150
prebuilt" if the gap is small enough that a blind listener
struggles to call it. The customs side has options — typing
feel, fit and finish, material storytelling, the social
contract of a group buy — and most of them are real value. But
the marketing line that customs sound better will have to
retire, and what replaces it isn't fully written yet.

The trend that runs under all three is the same one: acoustics
moved from a builder's outcome to a vendor's spec, and once
something becomes a spec the competition shifts to whatever
isn't specced yet. The [Trends Tracker](/trends/tracker) will
watch which lever the industry picks up next.
